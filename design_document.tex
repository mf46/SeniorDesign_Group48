\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Small-scale photovoltaic systems often lose harvest efficiency because a fixed solar panel cannot stay aligned with the direction of maximum irradiance throughout the day. A straightforward active tracker is not automatically a good solution, because every motor movement consumes energy. Under cloud cover, low-light conditions, or rapidly changing irradiance, the energy spent rotating the panel can exceed the extra electrical energy generated after the movement. Existing low-cost tracking solutions therefore fail to address the more important systems question: whether a movement produces positive \emph{net} energy gain after accounting for sensing, computation, and actuation cost.

This project addresses that gap by building a dual-axis photovoltaic tracking test platform that decides not only where to point the panel, but also whether moving is worth the energy cost. The result is a more realistic controller-evaluation platform for small distributed-energy scenarios, where energy-aware behavior matters more than maximizing motion.

\subsection{Solution Overview and Visual Aid}
The proposed system uses an STM32-centered hardware architecture with a Raspberry Pi-based RL inference layer above it. An STM32F407ZGT6 microcontroller on a dedicated lower-level control PCB handles real-time sensing, local motor control, safety checks, power measurement, and OLED status output. A dual-axis mechanical platform changes the solar panel yaw and pitch angles using `NEMA17` stepper motors driven through `A4988` stepper drivers. The current RL pipeline sends online state from the `STM32` to the Raspberry Pi, where the deployed model reads current light-ring information and the current two-axis angle, then predicts the next target angles for the two axes. The Raspberry Pi returns those target angles to the `STM32`, which executes them locally. In the current pipeline, the ``current angle'' used online is defined as the previous target angle accepted by the `STM32` after homing, not as an independently measured encoder value. Electrical telemetry is still measured and logged, but it is reserved for reward design, evaluation, and offline training rather than the online inference input itself. The final implementation is externally powered, with a `12~V` motor branch and a `5~V` logic branch, while the solar panel is measured only as a test source rather than used as the controller power rail. The light ring is implemented as a `BH1750 + mux + 16-sensor` array.

The target operating sequence is simple: after a single button press, the tracker performs self-check and homing, enters autonomous tracking mode, and continuously balances directional light information against measured panel output and measured motor cost. When ambient conditions make movement unprofitable, the controller pauses motion to avoid negative net gain.

\subsection{High-Level Requirements List}
The final system shall satisfy the following high-level requirements:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{Autonomous startup:} After one button press, the system shall complete self-check and homing, then enter autonomous tracking mode without requiring an external computer connection.
\item \textbf{Tracking and net-gain behavior:} Under stable lighting, the system shall align toward the brightest direction with steady-state pointing error below $5^\circ$. Under weak or rapidly varying light, the controller shall suspend unnecessary actuation and demonstrate non-negative net energy gain relative to a continuous-tracking baseline during the same test window.
\item \textbf{Telemetry and status visibility:} The OLED shall update at least twice per second and display system mode, solar-panel voltage, solar-panel current, and current yaw/pitch state.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Design}

\subsection{Block Diagram}
The system is divided into six functional blocks: online inference, embedded control, sensing, actuation, power, and user interface. The `STM32` uploads online state to the Raspberry Pi over a dedicated UART header, and the Raspberry Pi returns the next target angles for the two axes. The STM32-centered PCB integrates the sensing front end, I2C peripherals, motor-drive interface, and power-distribution network. The power subsystem uses a staged rail structure so that motor activity does not collapse the logic supply during startup or reversal. Offline training is performed on a PC or cloud host and is connected back into the system by exporting an `ONNX` model that is deployed to the Raspberry Pi.

\subsection{Mechanical and Actuation Subsystem}
The panel is mounted on a dual-axis frame with independent yaw and pitch motion. Yaw allows east-west reorientation, while pitch adjusts the panel elevation angle. Each axis is driven by a `NEMA17` stepper motor selected for adequate static torque and repeatable position control. The motors are driven by `A4988` stepper drivers under STM32 `STEP/DIR` control. In the current board mapping, the yaw axis uses `STEP` on `PA8` with direction or enable lines on `PB0` and `PB1`, while the pitch axis uses `STEP` on `PA9` with direction or enable lines on `PB10` and `PB11`.

This choice prioritizes sufficient holding torque, simple target-angle control, and a control path that can be realized on a student PCB. Because the project goal is energy-aware tracking rather than rapid motion, the design favors a stepper-based actuation path that keeps the software meaning of yaw and pitch angle straightforward. In the current control pipeline, the online ``current angle'' is defined as the last target angle accepted by the `STM32` after homing, so the RL path does not currently depend on a separate online encoder.

\textbf{Subsystem requirements}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Each axis shall support the mechanical travel required by the final structure, with a design target of at least $120^\circ$ yaw and $60^\circ$ pitch.
\item Each axis shall home successfully on startup using limit switches.
\item The stepper-driver stage shall operate within its current and thermal limits during repeated startup, reversal, and hold-position tests.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verification}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Measure available travel using a protractor or encoder count and confirm the required angular range.
\item Perform ten consecutive startup cycles and verify that homing completes successfully each time.
\item Record motor current during repeated motion tests at the PCB test points and verify that the chosen `A4988` configuration remains within safe operating limits.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sensing Subsystem}
The sensing subsystem provides both directional and energy-accounting information. A `BH1750 + mux + 16-sensor ring` is used to determine the relative direction and distribution of strongest light around the panel. The STM32 polls these digital light readings over I2C. `INA219_1` measures the solar-panel test path so the controller can estimate generated power under a controlled dummy load. `INA219_2` measures the motor branch so the system can estimate the electrical cost of actuation. These measurements allow the system to compare expected energy benefit against movement cost using data rather than a purely geometric estimate.

The combination of a light ring and direct electrical measurements reflects the actual design objective. Directional sensing alone is insufficient, because the brightest direction is not always worth pursuing if the extra generated energy is smaller than the energy consumed by movement.

\textbf{Subsystem requirements}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item The STM32 shall sample the `BH1750` light ring and both electrical telemetry channels at a rate of at least 10~Hz.
\item The power-sensing path shall resolve panel voltage and current well enough to distinguish meaningful changes in generated power during tracking tests.
\item Sensor values shall be filtered to prevent rapid oscillation caused by noise or transient shadows.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verification}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Log sensor samples while sweeping a light source across the panel and confirm that the light-ring pattern changes consistently with angle.
\item Compare measured panel voltage/current against a benchtop multimeter and confirm acceptable error for design-level energy comparison at several operating points.
\item Apply a moving-average or equivalent digital filter and verify that jitter in the motion command decreases without unacceptable lag.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Control and Communication Subsystem}
The control subsystem is centered on the STM32F407ZGT6. The `STM32` executes local control loops, motor driving, sensor acquisition, homing, and fault handling. In the current RL pipeline, the Raspberry Pi is the formal online inference node connected over a simple UART header. The `STM32` sends the model state needed for online inference, and the Raspberry Pi returns the next target angles for the two axes. Offline training remains outside the live control loop and is performed on a PC or cloud host.

At the board level, local peripherals use standard protocols: I2C for the OLED, both INA219 sensors, and the `BH1750` muxed light ring, plus `STEP/DIR` outputs for the `A4988` stepper-driver stage. In the current mapping, `PB6` and `PB7` are used for `SCL` and `SDA`. The UART header carries the RL control traffic between the `STM32` and Raspberry Pi: online state upload in one direction and target-angle commands in the other. The current online inference input is intentionally narrow, using only current light-ring information and current two-axis angle. Measured electrical telemetry remains in the logging path for reward calculation and offline evaluation.

\textbf{Subsystem requirements}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item The shared I2C bus shall support the OLED plus both INA219 devices without address conflict.
\item The `STM32` shall continue to enforce safe motor behavior independently of the Raspberry Pi inference node.
\item Loss of the UART link shall not leave the board in an unsafe motor state.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verification}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Scan the I2C bus and verify that the OLED and both INA219 devices respond at their intended addresses.
\item Disconnect the Raspberry Pi communication link and verify that the `STM32` exits or holds active motion safely.
\item Observe PWM and direction outputs for both axes and confirm that the expected logic state reaches the motor driver during forward, reverse, stop, and brake commands.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Power Subsystem}
The power subsystem must support both motor actuation and stable digital logic while also making energy flow measurable. In the current hardware design, an external `12~V` adapter powers the motor branch and both `A4988` drivers. A separate external `5~V` supply powers the Raspberry Pi and STM32 logic branch. The solar-panel output is routed through `INA219_1` into a dummy load so the panel output power can be measured independently of the controller power rails. `INA219_2` measures the motor branch supplied by the external `12~V` adapter. The power board should also include accessible test points for `12V`, `5V`, `3V3`, ground, I2C, UART, and motor supply so that the bring-up order can follow a safe sequence from power validation to full electromechanical integration.

\textbf{Subsystem requirements}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item The `5~V` logic rail shall remain within tolerance during motor startup and reversal.
\item The power subsystem shall provide telemetry needed to estimate generated electrical power.
\item The external `12~V` motor branch and external `5~V` logic branch shall be partitioned and protected against short-circuit, reverse-polarity, or over-current conditions.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verification}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Monitor the `5~V` logic rail during worst-case motor events and verify that the logic and display path do not brown out.
\item Compare sensor-based power estimates with multimeter measurements under several load and light conditions.
\item Validate the staged bring-up sequence by checking `12V`, `5~V`, `3.3~V`, I2C devices, and motor supply at dedicated test points before full system integration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{User Interface and One-Button Operation}
The local user interface is intentionally minimal. A single button triggers startup and homing. A 0.96-inch OLED shows mode, solar-panel power, motor-branch power, and yaw/pitch state so the team can verify operation without an attached laptop. The startup state machine is:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Power-on and self-test
\item Home yaw and pitch axes
\item Begin sensing and telemetry
\item Enter tracking or energy-saving hold mode
\end{enumerate}

This sequence directly supports the course requirement that the system function autonomously after a single button press.

\subsection{Tolerance Analysis}
One critical design tolerance is angular pointing error. For a flat solar panel under direct illumination, captured power decreases as panel normal misalignment increases. A simple approximation is

\begin{equation}
P(\theta) = P_{\max}\cos(\theta)
\end{equation}

where $\theta$ is the angle between the panel normal and the incoming dominant light direction. Under this model, a $5^\circ$ pointing error yields

\begin{equation}
\frac{P(5^\circ)}{P_{\max}} = \cos(5^\circ) \approx 0.996
\end{equation}

which corresponds to only about a $0.4\%$ reduction in ideal captured power. A $15^\circ$ error yields

\begin{equation}
\cos(15^\circ) \approx 0.966
\end{equation}

or about a $3.4\%$ reduction. This analysis motivates the control strategy: the system should avoid spending meaningful motor energy to correct very small angle errors, because the resulting energy improvement may be negligible compared with the actuation cost. Therefore, a deadband on motion commands is beneficial. The exact deadband can be tuned experimentally by comparing motor energy per movement against measured increase in panel output.

\section{Ethics and Safety}

\subsection{Ethics}
This project should present performance honestly. It would be misleading to claim that a motorized tracker always increases energy harvest. The entire purpose of the design is to test the opposite possibility and make decisions based on measured evidence. The team should therefore report both beneficial and non-beneficial operating cases and clearly distinguish between theoretical gain and measured net gain after actuation cost.

The team should also design for maintainability and safe operation. Communication protocols, control limits, and test procedures should be documented so future users do not operate the system in an unsafe or misleading way. These values are consistent with the IEEE Code of Ethics, particularly the obligations to be honest and realistic in stating claims, to improve understanding of technology, and to prioritize the safety, health, and welfare of the public.

\subsection{Safety}
The project involves rotating mechanical parts, external power supplies, and low-voltage electronics. Key safety controls include:

\begin{itemize}
\item limit switches and software travel limits to prevent over-rotation,
\item current-limited `A4988` drivers and protected wiring,
\item secure mounting of the solar panel and moving frame,
\item insulated external power connections and controlled common grounding,
\item low-voltage handling procedures during benchtop testing.
\end{itemize}

The team should verify that wires are strain-relieved, no conductive parts are exposed where accidental shorting is likely, and the panel mount remains stable during repeated movement. Testing should be performed on a bench or stand where accidental pinch points are visible and accessible.
