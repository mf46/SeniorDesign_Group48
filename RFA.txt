Problem: In modern microgrids, Distributed Energy Resources (DERs), particularly small-scale photovoltaic systems, suffer from significant efficiency losses due to the misalignment between solar panels and the sun. While dual-axis tracking systems exist, traditional active tracking methods often consume more power in actuation (motors) than they gain in generation, especially during intermittent cloud cover or low-light conditions. There is a lack of low-cost, adaptive control strategies that can autonomously evaluate the "net energy gain"—balancing the energy cost of moving against the potential generation revenue—in real-time. This project aims to address this pain point by appropriately scoping the problem and avoiding unnecessary system complexity.

Solution Overview: This project seeks to develop an intelligent, edge-computing-based control solution to maximize the net energy yield of a PV node, ensuring the technical and economic viability of small-scale microgrid applications. The system utilizes a Master-Slave architecture: a Raspberry Pi handles high-level computing (running net-energy optimization algorithms), while a microcontroller (e.g., STM32) manages hard-real-time motor execution. This approach not only fulfills the course's strict requirement for building functional hardware but also ensures that its complexity and modular design are highly feasible within the one-semester timeline and the team's available resources.

Components: The system is broken down into specific modules using a top-down approach:

Software Component:

Edge-Computing Logic: Intelligent algorithms (e.g., threshold-based or Q-Learning) deployed on the Raspberry Pi to determine optimal tracking strategies based on real-time irradiance and motor power consumption.

Embedded Control: Real-time code on the microcontroller for accurate sensor data acquisition, PWM generation for motors, and serial communication with the Raspberry Pi.

Data Visualization: Software to drive an OLED screen, dynamically displaying current voltage, net power gain, and system status.

Hardware Component:

Power & Control PCB: A custom-designed printed circuit board integrating a robust power management module (crucial for simultaneously powering the Raspberry Pi and motors via battery/PV), motor driver circuits, and sensor interfaces.

Core Computing Boards: Microcontroller (e.g., STM32/ESP32) and Raspberry Pi motherboard.

Sensor Array: Voltage/Current sensors (e.g., INA219) for power calculation, and photoresistors/LDRs for light tracking.

Mechanical Structure: Dual-axis pan-tilt mechanism, solar panel mounting bracket, and a stable chassis.

Criteria of Success:

One-Button Operation: The system must initiate self-calibration and enter autonomous tracking mode immediately upon a single button press, requiring no external computer connection throughout the process.

Basic Tracking: Under normal lighting conditions, the system can accurately identify and track the brightest light source.

Net-Energy Optimization: In simulated low-light or rapidly fluctuating light environments, the adaptive control algorithm must successfully pause motor actuation; it must demonstrate the avoidance of negative net energy gain compared to a continuous tracking baseline.

Data Visualization: The OLED display accurately and dynamically updates core system metrics (voltage, current, operating status).
